Voltage

The voltage or electric potential difference is the difference between the electric pontential in two points, which is equal to the work done per unit charge. Thus, to move 1 coloumb across 1 volt of electric potential requires 1 joule of work,

The voltage is measured in volts with symbol V. The volt is named in honour of the Italian physicist Alessandro Volta (1745–1827).

In the type of applications and projects described in this tutorial, there are two types of power sources providing voltages: direct current voltage (DC) power source or alternate current voltage (AC) power source. DC power sources provide constant voltage, examples are all type of batteries, voltage regulators, dynamos, AC/DC adapters (transform a AC signal into a DC signal with the appropriate voltage). AC power sources provide an alternate voltage (sinusoidal wave) typically provided by electric companies to consumers to power-up our electric appliances either with 110-120 volts (AC) or 220-240 volts (AC), but also in audio and radio signals.

Current

The current is the flow of an electric charge, for example moving electrons in a wire.

Current is measured in Ampères (A) and it is denoted with symbol I. The ampere is named in honour of the French physicist André-Marie Ampère (1775-1836).

The electrons flow in the opposite direction of the conventional electric current.


Resistor

A resistor is a passive component. In electronic circuits resistors have many purposes, such as reduce the current flow, adjust voltage levels, as voltage dividers, among others.

The resistance of resistors is measured in Ohms (Ω) and they are denoted with the symbol R.

Ohm's law states that the current through a resistor is proportional to its voltage V=I*R.

Resistors are usually fixed, but in many other cases the resitance of a component can vary upon a physic phenomena, for instance, an LDR is a light-dependent resistor with a variable resistance upon the amount of light received.

The total resistance of resistors connected in series is the sum of their individual resistance values R=R1+R2+...+Rn.

The total resistance of resistors connected in parallel is the reciprocal of the sum of the reciprocals of the individual resistors (1/R)=(1/R1)+(1/R2)+...+(1/Rn).
